How SJohnson Started on Roblox, Retired, and Found Inspiration in MDickie
When people hear the name SJohnson, they usually think about chaos, creativity, and unpredictable game ideas. But before any of that had a name, before there was a vision, there was just me sitting in front of a screen, trying to figure out how games were made.
And that's where Roblox came in.
How I Started Making Games on Roblox
I didn't start with some big master plan. I started because I was curious.
Like a lot of people, I first joined Roblox just to play. I was exploring other people's worlds, trying out random games, and thinking, "How did they even make this?" At some point, playing wasn't enough anymore. I wanted to build something of my own.
Roblox made that possible.
With Roblox Studio, I began experimenting. At first, it was simple things - placing blocks, building small maps, testing scripts I barely understood. I messed up a lot. Scripts didn't work. Characters glitched through walls. My "big ideas" turned into broken prototypes. But every mistake taught me something.
What hooked me wasn't just the building - it was the feeling of control. I could create a world from nothing. I could design how players moved, fought, survived, or explored. Even when my games weren't perfect, they were mine.
Over time, I started thinking bigger. I didn't just want to build random maps. I wanted style. I wanted identity. I wanted people to recognize my work and say, "Yeah, that's an SJohnson game."
Why I Retired from Making Games on Roblox
Retiring from Roblox wasn't about failure. It was about growth.
As I kept creating, I started to feel limited. Roblox is powerful, but it still has boundaries. I wanted more freedom - more control over mechanics, physics, art style, and how chaotic or realistic I could make things.
I also started realizing something important: I didn't just want to be a Roblox developer. I wanted to be a game creator in a bigger sense.
Roblox was my training ground. It taught me design basics, player psychology, how to think about gameplay loops, and how small details can change the way a game feels. But eventually, I felt like I had learned what I needed from it.
So I stepped away.
Not because I hated it. Not because it failed me. But because I was ready to move forward.
How MDickie Inspired Me
If Roblox taught me how to build, MDickie taught me how to be fearless.
MDickie's games are chaotic, unpredictable, and full of personality. Whether it's wrestling games, prison simulators, or superhero madness, his style is unmistakable. He doesn't try to make "perfect" games by industry standards. He makes games that feel alive.
That hit me hard.
His work showed me that you don't need photorealistic graphics or a massive studio. You need creativity. You need bold ideas. You need to take risks.
What inspired me most wasn't just his gameplay - it was his independence. He built his own lane. He didn't copy trends. He created his own style and stuck to it.
That made me rethink everything.
Instead of trying to make what I thought people expected, I started thinking: What would an SJohnson game look like if I didn't hold back?
That's when the vision shifted from "learning to make games" to "creating my own universe."
Where I Am Now
Roblox was the beginning. It gave me the foundation.
Retiring from it wasn't the end - it was the next level.
And MDickie's influence reminds me every time I create: don't play it safe. Don't chase perfection. Build something bold. Build something memorable. Build something that feels like chaos in motion.
SJohnson isn't just a name. It's a mindset.
It's about starting small, learning everything you can, and then stepping out of the box when it's time.

"Don't chase perfection. Chase fun - and build it your way."
